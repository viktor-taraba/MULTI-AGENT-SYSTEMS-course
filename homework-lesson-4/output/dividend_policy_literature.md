Title: Selected Scientific Articles on Dividend Policy — Annotated Bibliography

Introduction

This document lists peer-reviewed scientific articles that are central to dividend policy research. Each entry provides a standard citation and a short (2–4 sentence) summary of the article's purpose, methodology, and key findings. The list focuses on theoretical and empirical contributions across different paradigms (irrelevance, signaling, agency, taxation, cross-country evidence, and recent empirical patterns).

1. Lintner, J. (1956). "Distribution of Incomes of Corporations Among Dividends, Retained Earnings, and Taxes." The American Economic Review, 46(2), 97–113.
Summary: Lintner reports on firm-level interviews and time-series evidence to document how managers set dividends. He proposes the target payout and dividend-smoothing behavior: firms set a target payout ratio and adjust dividends gradually toward that target, explaining the observed persistence and slow adjustment of dividend changes.

2. Modigliani, F., & Miller, M. H. (1961). "Dividend Policy, Growth, and the Valuation of Shares." The Journal of Business, 34(4), 411–433.
Summary: Extending their earlier capital-structure irrelevance result, Modigliani and Miller derive conditions under which dividend policy is irrelevant for firm value in perfect capital markets. They show that with no taxes, transaction costs, or information asymmetry, investors are indifferent between dividends and capital gains; departures from the model motivate subsequent empirical work.

3. Black, F. (1976). "The Dividend Puzzle." The Journal of Portfolio Management, 2(2), 5–8.
Summary: Black highlights the apparent contradiction between theoretical indifference to dividends and the prominent role of dividends in corporate practice and investor preferences. The paper frames the "puzzle" that motivates later theoretical and empirical explanations (taxes, signaling, clientele, agency costs, catering).

4. Bhattacharya, S. (1979). "Imperfect Information, Dividend Policy, and ‘The Bird in the Hand’ Fallacy." The Bell Journal of Economics, 10(1), 259–270.
Summary: Bhattacharya develops a signaling model where dividends convey private information about future earnings; paying dividends can signal quality but may reduce retained funds. The model formalizes how information asymmetry can justify dividend payments despite apparent inefficiencies.

5. Miller, M. H., & Rock, K. (1985). "Dividend Policy under Asymmetric Information." The Journal of Finance, 40(4), 1031–1051.
Summary: Miller and Rock analyze optimal dividend and financing choices in a multi-period economy with managerial private information. They show how dividend decisions can be used to reveal information and affect external financing costs, producing empirically testable announcement effects.

6. Easterbrook, F. H. (1984). "Two Agency-Cost Explanations of Dividends." American Economic Review, 74(4), 650–659.
Summary: Easterbrook outlines two agency-cost rationales for dividends: (a) paying out cash reduces managers' free cash available for empire-building or value-destroying projects, and (b) dividends serve as a commitment device to mitigate agency conflicts with bondholders and outside investors. This work helped shift attention toward agency-based explanations.

7. Jensen, M. C. (1986). "Agency Costs of Free Cash Flow, Corporate Finance, and Takeovers." American Economic Review, 76(2), 323–329.
Summary: Jensen formalizes the free-cash-flow hypothesis: when firms generate large free cash flows and have poor investment opportunities, managers may invest suboptimally. Payouts (dividends or buybacks) and debt can discipline managers by reducing discretionary cash, linking payout policy to corporate governance and takeover incentives.

8. La Porta, R., Lopez-de-Silanes, F., Shleifer, A., & Vishny, R. (2000). "Agency Problems and Dividend Policies around the World." The Journal of Finance, 55(1), 1–33.
Summary: Using a large international sample, the authors test agency-based models and show that legal protections for minority shareholders strongly influence dividend policies. In countries where minority investors are poorly protected, firms pay higher dividends (disgorge cash) to reduce expropriation by insiders; the paper establishes an influential cross-country link between law, agency problems, and payout behavior.

9. Fama, E. F., & French, K. R. (2001). "Disappearing Dividends: Changing Firm Characteristics or Lower Propensity to Pay?" Journal of Financial Economics, 60(1), 3–43.
Summary: Fama and French document the dramatic decline in the fraction of U.S. firms paying dividends from 1978 to 1999. They decompose the decline into changing firm demographics (more young, high-growth listings) and a lower propensity to pay conditional on firm characteristics, suggesting tax, transaction-cost, and corporate-policy shifts as partial explanations.

10. Rozeff, M. S. (1982). "Growth, Beta and Agency Costs as Determinants of Dividend Payout Ratios." Journal of Financial Research, 5(3), 249–259.
Summary: Rozeff empirically links payout ratios to firm growth prospects, risk (beta), and agency-related ownership structure. The paper provides cross-sectional tests showing that firms with high growth, higher risk, and more concentrated insider ownership have systematically different payout behavior, supporting agency and lifecycle explanations.

11. Baker, M., & Wurgler, J. (2004). "A Catering Theory of Dividends." The Journal of Finance, 59(3), 1125–1165.
Summary: Baker and Wurgler introduce a behavioral/catering explanation: managers accommodate investor demand for dividend payers when sentiment/preferences make dividends more valuable. They show payout patterns are correlated with measures of investor sentiment and that catering helps explain time-varying dividend policies.

12. Aivazian, V. A., Booth, L., & Cleary, S. (2003). "Do Emerging Market Firms Follow Different Dividend Policies from U.S. Firms? Evidence from Eight Countries." Journal of Financial Research, 26(3), 371–387.
Summary: This paper compares dividend determinants in emerging markets versus the U.S., finding broadly similar drivers (profitability, leverage, investment opportunities) but structural differences in sensitivities. The authors document the role of asset mix and local financial constraints, indicating institutional context matters for payout behavior.

Concluding note

The selected articles span foundational theory (irrelevance, signaling), agency and governance explanations, empirical cross-sectional and cross-country evidence, and modern behavioral perspectives. Together they provide a compact but representative literature base for anyone researching dividend policy.
